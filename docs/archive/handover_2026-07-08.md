## 2. The Host Star

Stellar parameters come from the TESS Input Catalog version 8 (TIC v8; Stassun et al. 2019, AJ 158, 138). The TIC `lumclass` field reads "DWARF" but the physical parameters unambiguously point to an evolved subgiant-like host.

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Teff | 6332 ± 134 K |
| log g | 3.7075 ± 0.0835 |
| Rstar | 2.5926 ± 0.1094 Rsun |
| Mstar | 1.25 ± 0.19 Msun |
| rho_star | 0.0717 ± 0.0140 rho_sun |
| Distance | 201.96 ± 1.27 pc |
| Implied luminosity | ~9.7 Lsun |

The large radius, low surface gravity, and low mean density are physically inconsistent with a main-sequence dwarf, regardless of the TIC classification label. This evolved-host interpretation is scientifically central: the same radius ratio implies a companion ~2.2× larger around a 2.6 Rsun host than around a 1.0 Rsun host.

**2026-07-09 update — Spectroscopic archive search:** A programmatic 5-arcsec cone search of APOGEE DR17, LAMOST DR10, GALAH DR3, and RAVE DR6 returned zero matches. TIC 81077799 has never been observed by any major spectroscopic survey. All stellar parameters are photometric only (TIC v8 + Gaia GSP-Phot). See `spectroscopic_archives.json` and `spectroscopic_archives_summary.md`.

## 16. Publication Pathways and How to Get a DOI

### What a DOI Is

A DOI (Digital Object Identifier) is a permanent, globally unique link assigned to a document that makes it permanently citable, e.g. `10.3847/2515-5172/abcdef`. There are several ways to obtain one; which path is best depends on the goal.

---

### Option A — arXiv (No DOI, but permanent citable identifier)

**Acceptance: Certain (100%)**

arXiv is the standard preprint server for astrophysics. It does not issue DOIs; instead it issues a permanent arXiv identifier of the form `arXiv:2407.XXXXX`. This identifier is universally accepted as a citation in the field and is indexed in NASA ADS.

- **Cost**: Free
- **Review**: Human moderation only (checks basic scientific content, not quality)
- **Time**: Typically appears within 1–2 business days
- **URL**: https://arxiv.org — submit under `astro-ph.EP` (primary), cross-list `astro-ph.SR`

**This should be done first, regardless of any other path.** It establishes a permanent timestamp on the work and makes the paper citable immediately.

---

### Option B — Zenodo (Real DOI, immediate)

**Acceptance: Certain (100%)**

Zenodo is a free open-access repository operated by CERN/EU. It accepts any research output (papers, datasets, code) and issues a real, permanent DOI instantly. No peer review.

- **Cost**: Free
- **Review**: None
- **DOI format**: `10.5281/zenodo.XXXXXXX`
- **Time**: Immediate upon upload
- **URL**: https://zenodo.org

**Steps**:
1. Create a free Zenodo account
2. New Upload → upload `arxiv_main.pdf`
3. Fill in: Title, Author (Furkan Şen), Affiliation (Ankara University, Dept. of Astronomy and Space Sciences), Description (paste abstract), Keywords (exoplanets, TESS, transit photometry, planet candidates, evolved stars)
4. License: CC-BY 4.0 (standard for open science)
5. Click Publish → DOI assigned immediately

**Recommended**: Do arXiv + Zenodo on the same day. You then have both a community preprint record and a DOI-bearing archival record.

---

### Option C — Research Notes of the AAS / RNAAS (Peer-reviewed DOI)

**Acceptance: Likely, if condensed**

RNAAS is a peer-reviewed journal published by the American Astronomical Society. It is specifically designed for short observational notes — candidate characterizations, corrections to prior results, new measurements — which fits this paper's scope exactly. It is indexed in NASA ADS and counts as a real peer-reviewed publication.

- **Cost**: Free (open access)
- **Review**: Light peer review (~4–8 weeks)
- **DOI format**: `10.3847/2515-5172/XXXXXX`
- **Word limit**: ~1,000 words, 1–2 figures maximum
- **URL**: https://iopscience.iop.org/journal/2515-5172

**Critical constraint**: The current paper is 17 pages. RNAAS requires a separate, condensed version. The core finding to present would be:

> *"The independent TESS photometric analysis of TOI-3492.01 gives depth = 3167 ppm and Rp = 15.92 ± 0.68 Rearth, consistent with the official TOI scale. The candidate is giant-planet-sized around an evolved F-type subgiant."*

### 17.3 The a/R★ Tension — A Fresh Perspective

This is not just a limitation. It may be the most scientifically interesting result in the paper.

**What the tension actually means physically:**

The transit-derived a/R★ = 9.22 ± 0.28 implies a mean stellar density of ρ★ = 3M★/(4πa³/P²) ≈ 0.116 ρ☉. The TIC-predicted density from mass and radius is ρ★ ≈ 0.072 ρ☉. The transit "sees" a denser star than the TIC predicts.

There are four physical interpretations, ordered from most to least likely:

| Interpretation | What it means | Testable? |
|---|---|---|
| **TIC stellar parameters wrong** | The TIC v8 radius is overestimated (more main-sequence than subgiant) | Yes — spectroscopy + isochrone fitting |
| **Residual eccentricity** | Small e (even ~0.05) at appropriate ω can bias a/R★ high | Yes — RV |
| **Unmodeled third light** | A bound companion dilutes the transit, biasing geometry | Yes — AO imaging, RV trends |
| **Residual TESS systematics** | Sector-dependent flux offsets distort ingress/egress | Yes — sector-by-sector fits |

**The key insight**: The TIC lumclass=DWARF flag combined with the physical parameters (log g = 3.71, R★ = 2.59 R☉, ρ★ = 0.072 ρ☉) strongly suggests the TIC radius is poorly constrained. The TIC v8 uses SED fitting with broad priors; for evolved stars, the luminosity class can be ambiguous. If the true R★ is smaller (say 2.0 R☉ instead of 2.59 R☉), the TIC-predicted a/R★ would rise from 7.69 toward ~9+, potentially explaining the tension without any eccentricity or systematics.

**This is a stronger framing** than "the tension is an unresolved caveat." It is instead: "The a/R★ tension provides independent photometric evidence that the TIC stellar parameters may be unreliable for this target, consistent with the known difficulty of classifying evolved stars from broadband photometry alone. Spectroscopic confirmation of the stellar parameters is therefore the single most important follow-up observation — more immediately useful even than radial velocities."

### 17.4 The Cadence Artifact — Community Value

**This is undersold in the current paper.**

The fact that mixing 20 s and 120 s SPOC products in a single merged, flattened light curve suppresses transit depths by a factor of ~6 is not obvious. The mechanism is:
1. The 20 s products have 3× higher time resolution through ingress/egress
2. A flattening algorithm that treats all points equally will fit the transit wings as "baseline" at 20 s cadence precision
3. When the combined light curve is then normalized, the 120 s transit measurements are divided by a slightly-too-high baseline, reducing the apparent depth

This single sentence would substantially increase the paper's community impact — it warns other researchers about a potential trap.

### 17.5 The Statistical Validation Framing

**A nuanced problem with the current text.**

The paper previously mixed cautious candidate language with stronger formal-validation wording in the FPP section and Conclusions.

This was contradictory. Any wording that presents the object as already validated conflicts with the correct official TFOPWG disposition (PC = Planet Candidate).

**Recommendation**: The word "validated" should be used consistently and carefully:
- Use: "the signal passes standard statistical false-positive criteria (FPP < 0.1%)"
- Avoid language that implies confirmed planetary nature.
- The community standard for "statistical validation" (e.g., TRICERATOPS, VESPA) uses FPP < 1.5% as the threshold. The FPP = 0.011% clears this easily even with the simplified calculation — but the simplified nature of the calculation should not be hidden. A reviewer may push back on the claim of "validation" if it's based on a hand-rolled FPP.

The safest language: *"The signal passes strong statistical false-positive criteria: a simplified Morton-style calculation gives FPP ≈ 0.01%, and a TRICERATOPS screening run returns FPP numerically consistent with zero under the run assumptions. The object remains a candidate until RV confirmation or accepted validation-level follow-up."*

### 17.6 The Naming and Structure Problem (Editorial)

**What professional papers call their sections:**

## 19. Textbook and Methodology Insights for Improving the Article

*Added: 2026-07-08 (session 5). Based on analysis of "The Exoplanet Handbook" (Perryman 2018) and "Statistics, Data Mining, and Machine Learning in Astronomy" (Ivezic et al.).*

### 19.1 Astrodensity Profiling & The Photoeccentric Effect (Perryman 2018, §6.13)

One of the most powerful concepts in Perryman's *The Exoplanet Handbook* is **astrodensity profiling**—using the transit duration and shape to measure the mean stellar density $\rho_{\rm circ}$ under the assumption of a circular orbit. 

When a discrepancy arises between this photometric density and the true stellar density $\rho_\star$ (obtained via spectroscopy, isochrones, or asteroseismology), it is known as the **photoeccentric effect** (Dawson & Johnson 2012). The relationship is governed by:

$$\frac{\rho_{\rm circ}}{\rho_\star} = \frac{(1 + e\sin\omega)^3}{(1 - e^2)^{3/2}}$$

For TOI-3492.01, the MCMC transit fit yields a scaled semimajor axis of $a/R_\star = 9.22 \pm 0.28$, while the TIC stellar density predicts $a/R_\star = 7.69 \pm 0.50$ for a circular orbit. 
This density discrepancy is:

$$\frac{\rho_{\rm circ}}{\rho_\star} = \left(\frac{9.22}{7.69}\right)^3 \approx 1.72$$

**Strategic Improvement**: Rather than framing this $2.6\sigma$ tension as an unexplained system limitation, can use the photoeccentric equation to make a quantitative physical prediction:
- A moderate orbital eccentricity of $e \approx 0.20$ oriented with the argument of periastron near periastron passage ($\omega \approx 90^\circ$) would perfectly resolve the density discrepancy.
- This gives a concrete, testable hypothesis for radial velocity follow-up. This analytical explanation should be added to the Discussion section of the manuscript.

### 19.2 Transit Parameter Degeneracies at High Impact Parameter (Perryman 2018, §6.13.4)

Perryman emphasizes that transits with high impact parameters ($b \gtrsim 0.7$) suffer from severe degeneracies:
- As $b$ increases, the transit becomes more V-shaped.
- The transit model can fit a V-shape either by increasing the impact parameter $b$ and increasing $a/R_\star$ (making the transit speed faster to match the observed duration), or by changing the limb darkening.
- In the fit, the impact parameter is high ($b = 0.788 \pm 0.015$). The strong diagonal correlation between $b$ and $a/R_\star$ in the corner plot is a direct consequence of this degeneracy.

**Strategic Improvement**: The transit fitting discussion should explain that the high fitted $a/R_\star$ is partially degenerate with the high impact parameter $b$. To break this degeneracy in future analyses, a Gaussian prior on the stellar density (and thus on $a/R_\star$) could be applied using spectroscopic host-star parameters, forcing the MCMC to explore the degenerate space under physical constraints.

---

### 19.3 Limb Darkening Sensitivity Vetting (Perryman 2018, §6.14.1)

Perryman notes that fixing limb-darkening coefficients to theoretical tables without sensitivity testing can introduce systematic errors in the derived planet-to-star radius ratio $R_p/R_\star$.

**Strategic Improvement**: This vetting has already been completed! The re-analysis using exact Claret (2017) coefficients from `LDTk` ($u_1 = 0.393, u_2 = 0.150$) versus the legacy approximate values shifted the posteriors by less than $0.1\sigma$. This null result should be explicitly reported in the manuscript to demonstrate to the referee that the derived radius ($1.59 \pm 0.68 R_\oplus$) is robust against limb-darkening systematic errors.

### 19.4 Rigorous Model Selection and MCMC Convergence (Ivezic et al., §5.8)

From Ivezic's *Statistics, Data Mining, and Machine Learning in Astronomy*, two rigorous enhancements can be drawn:

1. **Information Criteria for Orbit Model Selection**:
   - The circular model ($e=0$) has 4 free parameters ($R_p/R_\star$, $a/R_\star$, $b$, and baseline).
   - The eccentric model has 6 free parameters (adding $e\sin\omega$ and $e\cos\omega$).
   - The Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) can be computed: $\mathrm{BIC} = \chi^2 + k \ln N$, where $k$ is the number of parameters and $N = 102,502$ data points.
   - Because the eccentric model's MCMC chain failed to converge well and did not significantly improve the fit, the circular model has a much lower BIC (due to the $k \ln N$ penalty for 2 extra parameters). This provides a solid statistical justification for adopting the circular model.

2. **Multi-Chain MCMC Convergence Diagnostics**:
   - Instead of relying on a single chain's integrated autocorrelation time ($\tau$), Ivezic recommends running multiple independent chains starting from widely separated positions and computing the Gelman-Rubin diagnostic ($\hat{R}$).
   - A multi-chain fit with $\hat{R} < 1.05$ should be recommended as a standard follow-up step to solidify the MCMC parameters.
